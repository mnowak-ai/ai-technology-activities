BWhat shifts now

A second opinion — from AI

In Phase A, you worked through three incidents using your own thinking. Now you'll hand those same cases to an AI tool and see how it approaches them. The goal isn't to see who got it right — it's to understand where AI helps and where it falls short.

AI can move fast and surface things you might miss. But it also makes assumptions, misses context, and sometimes confidently suggests the wrong thing. Your job is to compare, correct, and improve.

Starter prompt
Analyze these three system incidents from an IT support team. For each case, identify the most likely cause of the problem. Suggest two or three possible explanations. Estimate how serious each issue is on a low, medium, or high scale. Explain your reasoning in simple, clear language — no technical jargon.
"What is the single most likely cause for each issue?"
"Which of these could be resolved quickly, and which might take longer to fix?"
"Could any of these three incidents be connected to each other?"

3Read it carefully

What AI may do — well, and not

Before you compare its output to your own, keep both of these in mind.

AI often handles well

→Identifying common, well-known causes for system slowdowns.
→Generating several possible explanations quickly.
→Spotting connections between cases that look similar.
→Suggesting logical next steps for investigation.

Where AI can slip

!Assuming technical complexity when the cause is simple.
!Missing workplace context (the end-of-month deadline, the policy change).
!Treating every login failure as a security threat.
!Sounding certain about things it can't actually know.
"

AI gives interpretation — not truth. The context you bring is something it cannot have.
