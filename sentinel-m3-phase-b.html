Documentation prompt — use this exactly
Take the incident documentation above and do three things. First, rewrite it as a professional IT support report with clear sections, improved formatting, and better readability for each incident. Second, create a simple standard operating procedure for each type of incident — short numbered steps for identifying the problem, responding to it, and preventing it in future. Third, write a brief internal knowledge base entry for each incident — two to three sentences that a team member could read quickly to understand what happened and what to watch for. Please keep all language simple and free of technical jargon.

AI will produce three types of documents. Here's what each one is for:

Incident Report
Formal record

A structured summary of what happened — used for internal review, audits, and team debriefs.

SOP
Operational guide

Step-by-step instructions for handling this type of incident in the future — used by support teams during live shifts.

Knowledge Base Entry
Quick reference

A short summary written for the whole organization — readable in seconds, used for training and prevention.

3Before you read the output

What AI does with documentation — well, and not

Documentation tasks bring out both AI's strengths and its most common mistakes. Keep both columns close as you review.

AI often handles well

→Restructuring scattered notes into clear, readable sections.
→Adding professional headings, labels, and document structure.
→Generating numbered SOP steps from a general description.
→Writing brief, clear knowledge base summaries from longer notes.

Where AI tends to slip

!Adding specific details that were never in your original notes.
!Turning uncertain causes into confirmed explanations.
!Writing SOPs for situations it invented or assumed.
!Sounding confident where your notes said "unclear" or "unknown."
"

If AI adds details that were not in the original notes, they must be corrected.

1Incident Report— AI's formal summary of all three incidents
Model answer

A strong response notes that the AI organized the notes into clear headed sections, added consistent formatting across all three incidents, and made the report easier to scan than the original raw notes.

Model answer

A strong response catches specific overreach — for example, if the AI states a cause as confirmed when the original notes said "likely but unconfirmed," or if it adds a detail (a number, a name, a location) that wasn't in the original documentation at all. Naming the exact sentence that oversteps is more useful than a general complaint.

2Standard Operating Procedure— AI's step-by-step handling guide
Model answer

A strong response checks each step against what's actually known — flagging any step that assumes a confirmed cause, references a team or tool that was never mentioned, or gives an analyst instructions they couldn't realistically follow with the information on hand.

3Knowledge Base Entry— AI's quick-reference summaries
Model answer

A strong response is specific about which entries are safe to publish as-is and which aren't — for example, flagging a knowledge base entry that states a security concern as fact when the original notes only considered it a possibility. Overconfident language in a KB entry misleads more people than the same overreach in an internal report.

Sentinel AI-Enhanced Documentation Package

Final Incident Report
Corrected + human-verified formal record
Rewrite or edit the AI's incident report so every statement is accurate, every uncertainty is honest, and no unsupported details remain.
Model answer

A strong final report keeps the AI's improved structure and formatting but restores every uncertainty the AI smoothed over — using language like "likely" or "not confirmed" wherever that matches the original Phase A notes, and removing any specific detail that wasn't in those notes.

Final SOP Document
Corrected step-by-step operational guide
Edit the AI's SOP so the steps reflect what an analyst could actually do — based on what you know, not what the AI assumed. Remove any steps that require information that isn't confirmed.
Model answer

A strong final SOP keeps only steps an analyst could actually execute with confirmed information — removing steps that assume an unconfirmed cause or reference resources that were never part of the original incident.

Final Knowledge Base Entry
Corrected quick-reference summaries for all teams
Write the final version of each knowledge base summary — 2–3 sentences per incident, accurate, honest about uncertainty, and ready for any Sentinel team member to read.
Model answer

A strong final knowledge base entry is 2–3 honest sentences per incident that state what's known, name what's still uncertain, and avoid alarming language the original notes don't support.
