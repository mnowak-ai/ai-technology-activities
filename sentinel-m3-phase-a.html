1Before you write

Three principles of good documentation

Keep these in mind as you work through each incident. Good documentation passes one test: will another analyst understand this without asking you a single question?

Be clear

Use plain language. If a word feels technical, swap it for a simpler one. Pretend you're explaining it to someone on their first day.

"Would someone with no background understand this?"

Be brief

Write only what's needed. Long documentation gets ignored. Short, well-organized documentation gets used. Cut anything that doesn't help someone take action.

"Does every sentence earn its place?"

Be useful

Think about why someone will read this. Future training? Preventing a repeat? Make sure the document serves that purpose directly.

"What will someone do differently after reading this?"

1 System Performance Slowdown ✓ Resolved
INC-0241 · Government Services Portal
The system slowed noticeably during a busy reporting period. It recovered on its own. No users lost data. The likely cause was too many people logged in at the same time, but this was never confirmed.
1
Incident Summary — what happened?
2–3 sentences. Write it as if you're telling a colleague who was out that day.
2
Observed behavior — what did users see or experience?
Focus on what was visible, not what was happening behind the scenes.
3
Impact — who was affected, and how much?
How disruptive was this — minor, moderate, or significant? Who noticed?
4
Resolution — how did it end?
Did someone intervene, or did it resolve on its own? Be honest about what's still uncertain.
5
Key takeaway — what should Sentinel remember?
One or two sentences. What should be monitored or done differently next time?
Clarity check: Could another analyst understand this incident from your notes alone — without seeing the original report?
2 Login & Access Issues ✓ Resolved
INC-0242 · Hospital Records System
Several users could not log in on the first try. The most likely explanation was that a password policy change wasn't communicated clearly — some users didn't know their old password no longer worked. No confirmed security issue. One user logged in after hours.
1
Incident Summary — what happened?
Describe the issue briefly. Mention the setting — hospital records — because context matters for future readers.
2
Observed behavior — what did users see or experience?
What happened on screen? What did users report to support?
3
Impact — how disruptive was this?
Did it affect patient records access? Were users locked out completely or just delayed?
4
Resolution — how did it end?
Were users able to regain access? Was a root cause confirmed?
5
Key takeaway — what should Sentinel remember?
What process change or monitoring approach might prevent this next time?
Clarity check: Would the next analyst understand why users couldn't log in — and what the likely cause was — just from your notes?
3 Unexpected System Behavior ✓ Stabilized
INC-0243 · School District File System
Some users started seeing an intermittent error when saving files. Others doing the same tasks had no problems. A folder reorganization two days earlier may have caused some accounts to lose access to new folder locations, but this was never fully confirmed. The issue stabilized on its own.
1
Incident Summary — what happened?
This one is messier — be honest about the uncertainty. Good documentation admits what wasn't known.
2
Observed behavior — what stood out as unusual?
The inconsistency is the key detail here — describe it clearly.
3
Impact — how much did this affect users?
Inconsistent issues are often harder for users to report and harder for support to track.
4
Resolution — how did it end?
Be transparent about unresolved questions — it matters for future reference.
5
Key takeaway — what should Sentinel flag for the future?
Especially for cases like this where the cause is unclear, what should be checked or monitored going forward?
Clarity check: Is the uncertainty clearly stated — so future readers know this wasn't fully resolved and still needs attention?
